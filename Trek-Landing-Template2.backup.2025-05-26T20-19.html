Best Time to Trek

Weather plays a crucial role in your Everest Base Camp experience. Choose your season wisely.

🌸

Spring

March - May

Rhododendrons in full bloom, clear mornings with afternoon clouds. This is peak climbing season, so you might see expeditions preparing for summit attempts.

🍂

Autumn

September - November

Crystal clear skies and stable weather make this the most popular trekking season. Expect busy trails but spectacular mountain views.

❄️

Winter

December - February

Very cold but clear skies and fewer trekkers. Some lodges may be closed higher up. Only for experienced cold-weather trekkers.

🌧️

Monsoon

June - August

Not recommended due to daily rain, cloudy skies obscuring views, and muddy, leech-infested trails. Flights to Lukla often cancelled.

Monthly Weather Overview

Day Temperatures: 10°C to 15°C (lower at higher altitudes)

Night Temperatures: -10°C to 0°C at Base Camp

Precipitation: Minimal in autumn, occasional snow in spring

Visibility: Best in autumn, good in spring mornings

Health & Safety

Your safety is paramount. Understanding the risks and proper preparation is essential.

⚠️

Altitude Sickness Risk - High

The trek reaches 5,364m. Proper acclimatization is essential. Symptoms include headache, nausea, fatigue, and dizziness. Ascend slowly, stay hydrated, and descend if symptoms worsen.

ℹ️

Medical Facilities

Basic health posts in Lukla and Namche. HRA Aid Post in Pheriche offers altitude sickness treatment. Helicopter evacuation available (ensure your insurance covers up to 6,000m).

Recommended Vaccinations

  • ✓ Hepatitis A & B
  • ✓ Typhoid
  • ✓ Japanese Encephalitis
  • ✓ Rabies (if extended stay)
  • ✓ Routine vaccinations up to date

Common Health Issues

  • • Khumbu cough (dry cough from altitude)
  • • GI problems (traveler's diarrhea)
  • • Sun exposure (intense UV at altitude)
  • • Dehydration
  • • Blisters

Sherpa Culture & Local Cuisine

Immerse yourself in the rich Tibetan Buddhist culture of the Khumbu region.

Cultural Etiquette

  • 🔄 Always walk clockwise around mani stones and stupas
  • 👟 Remove shoes before entering monasteries
  • 🚫 Don't touch religious artifacts or paintings
  • 📸 Ask permission before photographing people
  • 👔 Dress modestly, especially in religious sites
  • 🤝 Use right hand for giving and receiving

Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to the most common questions about the Everest Base Camp trek.

Do I need previous trekking experience? +
No, but good physical fitness is essential. The trek is non-technical but demanding due to altitude and duration. If you can hike 6-8 hours daily and have good cardiovascular fitness, you can complete this trek. Consider doing some practice hikes with a loaded backpack before you go.
Can I trek independently? +
Yes, it's allowed, but hiring a guide is highly recommended for safety, navigation, and cultural insights. The trail is well-marked, but altitude sickness is a real risk, and having an experienced guide who can recognize symptoms and know when to descend can be lifesaving. Guides also handle logistics and provide valuable cultural context.
What about altitude sickness? +
It's a real risk above 3,000m. Follow the itinerary's acclimatization days, stay hydrated (3-4 liters daily), and descend if symptoms worsen. Common symptoms include headache, nausea, fatigue, and difficulty sleeping. Consider taking Diamox (consult your doctor first). The key is to ascend slowly - the trek itinerary is designed with proper acclimatization in mind.
Is travel insurance mandatory? +
While not legally required, it's essential. Ensure it covers trekking up to 6,000m and helicopter evacuation. Many standard travel insurance policies exclude high-altitude trekking, so read the fine print. Helicopter evacuation can cost $4,000-6,000, so proper coverage is crucial. Some companies specialize in adventure travel insurance.
What if I can't get a flight to/from Lukla? +
Weather delays are common. Budget 2-3 buffer days in Kathmandu. During peak season, flights sometimes get diverted to Ramechhap (4 hours from Kathmandu by road). Helicopter shuttles are available but expensive. The overland route via Salleri/Phaplu is more reliable but adds 2-3 days each way to your trek.
